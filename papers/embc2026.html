Structure-aware Adaptive Kernel MPPCA Denoising for Diffusion MRI
Ananya Singhal  ·  Dattesh Dayanand Shanbhag  ·  Sudhanya Chatterjee
GE HealthCare, Advanced Technology Group, Bangalore, India
EMBC 2026 · Engineering in Medicine and Biology Society · Toronto, Canada · Invention Disclosure — GE HealthCare
Abstract
We present a structure-aware adaptive kernel denoising method for high b-value diffusion MRI that dynamically adjusts denoising kernel geometry per-voxel using gradient-informed structural priors. Standard MPPCA denoising methods apply fixed kernel sizes uniformly across the brain — a critical limitation in oncology applications where preserving sharp tumor boundaries is clinically essential while simultaneously requiring aggressive noise suppression in surrounding tissue. Our method clusters voxel-wise gradient magnitudes via k-means and monotonically assigns discrete odd-integer kernel sizes, generating a per-voxel kernel map that autonomously adapts MPPCA denoising to local anatomical complexity. On HCP (MGH-USC) data at b-values of 5000–10000 s/mm², the method delivers an 18% SNR gain with 96% tissue boundary preservation versus fixed-size MPPCA baselines.
Motivation
High b-value diffusion MRI (b ≥ 5000 s/mm²) is essential for probing restricted diffusion in dense cellular environments — enabling axonal diameter estimation, tumour microstructure characterisation, and white matter pathology quantification at sub-micron resolution. However, signal-to-noise ratio degrades catastrophically at high b-values due to the T2 decay of the diffusion-attenuated signal, making denoising a prerequisite for clinical utility.

Existing denoising methods — including Marchenko-Pastur PCA (MPPCA), NYU-MPPCA, DIPY-MPPCA, and adaptive patching approaches — apply fixed kernel sizes uniformly across the image volume. This is fundamentally at odds with the heterogeneous anatomical landscape of the brain: a large kernel that effectively suppresses noise in homogeneous white matter will simultaneously blur the sharp boundaries between tumor core, infiltrated tissue, and healthy parenchyma — boundaries that are critical for surgical planning and margin delineation.

What is needed is a denoising method that is simultaneously aggressive where tissue is homogeneous and conservative where structural boundaries are present — adapting autonomously to local anatomical complexity without requiring manual parameter tuning.
Contributions
  1. Gradient-informed structural prior: a per-voxel measure of local tissue anisotropy derived from the gradient magnitude of the b=0 reference image, capturing tissue boundary information without requiring explicit segmentation.
  2. Gradient-to-kernel mapping algorithm: k-means clustering of voxel-wise gradient magnitudes into discrete bins with monotonic assignment to odd-integer MPPCA kernel sizes — small kernels (3×3×3) for high-gradient boundary voxels, large kernels (7×7×7 or larger) for low-gradient homogeneous regions.
  3. Per-voxel kernel map generation spanning the full brain volume, enabling autonomous adaptation of MPPCA denoising to local anatomical complexity without manual tuning and without requiring retraining.
  4. Delivers 18% SNR gain with 96% tissue boundary preservation versus fixed-size MPPCA baselines (NYU-MPPCA, DIPY-MPPCA) and adaptive patching methods on HCP (MGH-USC) data at b-values of 5000–10000 s/mm²; produces cleaner DWI volumes and more accurate colour FA maps.

Evaluated on the Human Connectome Project MGH-USC dataset at b-values of 5000, 7500, and 10000 s/mm², our voxel-adaptive method outperforms NYU-MPPCA, DIPY-MPPCA, and adaptive patching approaches on both SNR metrics and tissue boundary fidelity scores. Visual inspection confirms preserved tumor-to-normal tissue contrast in oncology cases and sharper white matter tract boundaries in tractography-relevant regions. The method requires no retraining and runs on standard GPU hardware with per-subject runtime comparable to fixed-kernel MPPCA.
